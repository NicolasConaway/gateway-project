Imperial Palace

Where once stood the Edo castle now stands the Imperial Palace. After the Tokugawa shogun was overthrown in 1868, the construction of the new Imperial palace began and ended in 1888. This new palace was destroyed at one point during World War II, but later reconstructed as the same. It is the home of the current Imperial Family. The tour gives a look around the outside and it's gardens but you are not allowed to go inside. Guests of all ages are welcome to the tour and are able to appreciate

Admission Fee:

  • There are no admission fees to participate in the tour, but reservations must be made well in advance.
  • For all ages

Tokyo National Museum

As well as the oldest it is also the largest national museum that Japan all together has to offer. Ranging around 4,000 items to view inside the museum permanently with addition of temporary items to encase yourself in. The museum will appeal to older teens to adult, visitors that are old enough to appreciate the fine and traditional arts.

Admission Fee:

  • $5.65 (or 620 yen) for the permanent displays
  • $13.66 (or 1500 yen) for the limited time displays.
  • For all ages

Tokyo Tower

Modeled after the Effiel Tower, this 333 meter broadcast antenna stands strong in the middle of Tokyo as an icon of Japan's post-war rebirth. You are able to go as high as 150 meters up to the first deck, or you could go up another 100 meters, making that 250 meters in the air to the top deck. On the top deck guests are able to get a view of Mt.Fuji off in the distance. This tourist sight is for everyone of all ages to enjoy as long as you have no fear of height.

Admission Fee:

  • $10.93 (or 1200 yen) to go to the first deck
  • $27.32 (or 3000 yen) to get access to both decks
  • For all ages

This Disney resort is made up of 7 ports all based off the myths and legends of the sea. Going clockwise from the enterance, these ports consist of the Mediterranean Harbor, Mysterious Island, American Waterfront, Port Discovery, Lost River Delta, Mermaid Lagoon, Arabian Coast. Though people of any age are able to enjoy the park, it was originally targeting adults, due to the wider selection or sit-down resturants and serving of alcohol.

Ginza District

With many stores, cafes, resturants, night clubs and more, the Ginza district is the most famous of Tokyo's shopping center. Being that it is the most famous shopping center it is also the most expensive real estate that Japan has to offer. Adults, elderly and kids will all be satisfied while shopping or dining within Ginza. This district's icon is the Ginza Wako, a clock tower in the center of Ginza that sells luxury wares within it.

Meiji Shrine

This shrine was made for and named after Emperor Meiji, who moved to Tokyo after the end of the Tokugawa era. Emperor is an important figure of Japan, because he was the fist emperor of modern Japan. During World War II the shrine was destroyed and was later rebuilt. This shrine has no specific target appeal other than to respect the late Emperor. Recently built as part of the shrine is a new museum called the Meiji Jingu Museum that displays items from the shrine's collection.

Admission Fee:

  • Free to enter the shrine
  • $9.10 (or 1000 yen) for museum access
  • For all ages

Akihabara

Another shopping district to enjoy, but this time it revolves around electronics, animated movies and anime. Akihabara is known for its various electronics shops, but when you see and step inside the district you'll see that it's littered in anime billboards. Because of this recent recognition, there are many shops devoted for this gnere and group of people who favor these goods along with their electronic stores. As long as you enjoy technology and anime, your age does not matter when you're wandering around this shopping district.
